Pattern Break

The Toolkit

Practical tools for choosing what to work on — and how. Browse, pick what sparks, bring it to the room.

You don't need to do all of this. You don't even need to do most of it. Scan the categories below. Notice what pulls you — what creates a little spark or a little resistance. That's your starting point. Bring it to a session, or start working with it on your own.

Each tool expands to show you exactly how to use it. Some are five-minute exercises. Others are orientations you carry into every interaction. Mix and match.

Pattern Breaks

The core mechanic. Find your defaults, identify their opposite, and practice the stretch. If both could be your pattern, neither one is "you" — and you're freer than you were.

The foundational move. You identify a default way you show up, then practice its exact opposite — not to replace one with the other, but to see that neither is fixed. When you can do both, the pattern loses its grip.

  1. Name a default: "I'm the one who stays quiet." "I always take care of everyone." "I never ask for what I want."
  2. Identify the 180°: What's the exact opposite? "I speak first." "I let others take care of me." "I state my desire out loud."
  3. Imagine doing it. Let yourself feel the discomfort, the resistance, the "that's not me" reaction.
  4. Role play it with a practice buddy. Start small and safe.
  5. Increase the intensity. Practice it guerrilla-style in low-stakes real situations.
  6. Bring it into the room. Use it when the situation calls for it.
  7. Keep it as a tool — not a new identity, but an available choice.
Ask yourself: "How would someone describe me?" Then ask: "What's the opposite of each of those things?"

When you're stuck and don't know which direction to go, any disruption will do. These are pattern breaks you can use right now, without any self-knowledge required. The point isn't the action — it's the interruption of the automatic.

  1. Put your finger up your nose. (Seriously. It's a pattern break. The absurdity is the point.)
  2. Do something from Everything Everywhere All At Once — anything strange, non-sequitur, silly, out of character.
  3. Say the thing you least want to say out loud.
  4. Move your body in a way that doesn't match the emotion you're in.

These work because they break the trance of "this is just how it is." Once the trance breaks, you can choose.

These require knowing yourself — or at least being willing to look. You're identifying the specific places where you are stuck, and designing specific interventions.

  1. Identify your defaults or "stuck" places. Ask a trusted person: "What do you think I should change?" or ask yourself: "What isn't working for me anymore?"
  2. Name them clearly: "I always defer." "I get defensive when challenged." "I disappear when things get intense."
  3. Find the 180° of each one.
  4. Practice the 180° through the escalation ladder: imagine → role play → low-stakes real life → bring it to the room.
  5. Integrate: this isn't your new personality. It's a tool in the belt for the jobs it's needed for.

A more advanced orientation. When you practice enough 180°s, you start to see that your personality is a collection of patterns you've identified with. Once you see this clearly, you no longer need to either obey the patterns or rebel against them. You just do what the situation calls for.

The pattern might say you should be angry, but it's obvious that anger isn't the useful response — so you just don't. Not because you're suppressing. Because you're not identified with it anymore.

This often emerges naturally from committed practice. You can't skip to it, but you can recognize when you're there.

Relational Range

Expand what's available to you in how you show up with other people. The wider your range, the more situations you can navigate with grace — and the less you're at the mercy of one way of being.

Make a list of qualities you default to, and their opposites. This becomes your personal training menu — a map of the edges where growth lives.

Quiet ↔ Loud  ·  Leading ↔ Following  ·  Accommodating ↔ Demanding  ·  Gentle ↔ Fierce  ·  Certain ↔ Uncertain  ·  Visible ↔ Hidden  ·  Serious ↔ Playful  ·  Controlling ↔ Surrendered

Pick one pair per session. Practice the side you rarely inhabit.

Pick a character — from a movie, a book, someone you admire, an archetype — and try being them. Not performing. Inhabiting. Feel what it's like to move through the world with their qualities available to you.

  1. Choose someone whose way of being fascinates or intimidates you.
  2. Identify 2-3 specific qualities they embody that you don't (yet).
  3. In a session or conversation, let those qualities move through you. How would they respond? What would they say? How would they hold their body?
  4. Notice what shifts in how people respond to you.

If the character you're drawn to is someone whose power scares you — that's gold. That's probably the edge you need.

In any interaction, there's a dance of who leads, who follows, and who has final say. Most of us default to one position. The practice is learning to move fluidly between all three.

  1. Notice your default: Do you typically lead? Follow? Defer on the final call? Take it?
  2. In your next interaction, consciously choose the opposite position.
  3. Practice leading when you normally follow — make proposals, set direction, take up space.
  4. Practice following when you normally lead — let someone else's idea carry, support their vision, let go of control.
  5. Practice having final say when you normally defer — or practice releasing final say when you normally hold it.
The question isn't "which am I?" but "which do I have access to right now, and which am I afraid of?"

Consciously practicing the impact you have on another person. Not manipulating — experimenting, with their knowledge and consent. Can you make someone feel safe? Seen? Challenged? Delighted? Can you do it on purpose?

  1. Choose an impact you want to practice creating (e.g., warmth, groundedness, challenge, playfulness).
  2. Notice what you do — voice, body, eye contact, words, pacing — when you're going for that impact.
  3. Ask the other person: "What impact am I actually having on you?" The gap between your intent and their experience is where the learning lives.
  4. Adjust. Try again. Develop fluency.
"You only need the example of one person doing the thing you want to do — to know you're not alone in the pursuit."

Desire

The fuel. Most of us have been trained out of knowing what we want — or scared out of saying it. These tools help you reconnect with desire and practice outing it, which is where aliveness lives.

A structured way to dig into what you want when you come up blank. Desire isn't always loud. Sometimes you have to sneak up on it.

"What do I want?" → blank? → "What do I want more than I want to put my finger up my nose?" → keep going. Choose a random starting point and ask what you'd want more than that. Keep raising the bar until something lands.
  1. Ask: "What do I want?" and wait. Really wait.
  2. If blank: use the "more than" ladder. Pick something absurd and ask what you'd want more.
  3. Ask: "In what areas do I already know exactly what I want? What does that clarity feel like?" Look for that same feeling elsewhere.
  4. Ask: "What would it feel like to know what I wanted?" Can you feel that right now?
  5. Ask: "What do I want that I'm scared to own? What's the bad thing I think would happen if I said it?"

Desire operates at different time horizons. Zoom in and out. Sometimes the daily want reveals the decade want, and sometimes it's the reverse.

What do I want today? This week? This month? This quarter? This year? In five years? In ten? In twenty?

Don't force answers. Some timelines will feel alive and some will feel blank. The blank ones are interesting too.

The practice of saying what you want while you're with other people. Not after. Not in your journal. Right then. This is what makes Pattern Break different from solo work — the real-time risk of being known.

  1. In a session, notice when you want something — attention, connection, challenge, to be left alone, to be held, to play.
  2. Say it out loud. "I want…" "I'm noticing I want…" "What I actually want right now is…"
  3. Notice what happens in your body when you say it. Notice what happens between you and the other person.
  4. Repeat. The practice is the repetition, not the perfection.

Feeling the Feels

When you break patterns — or just live life — feelings come up. Riding them to completion soothes the system, reduces their intensity, and loosens your identification with them. Here's how.

  1. Let it arise. Get in touch with the feeling. Don't chase it — let it find you.
  2. Allow it to move. Emotions want to complete themselves. Give them space — through verbal expression, letting the feeling flow, body movement, or facial expression.
  3. Don't stop early. The temptation is to cut it off when it peaks. Stay with it. It will crest and begin to dissolve on its own.
  4. Notice the aftermath. What's here now? Often something quieter, clearer, and more true than the emotion itself.

A way to find your feelings when you don't know what you're feeling. You try on labels and feel for resonance — like tuning a radio.

  1. Say "I feel ___" out loud (or internally), trying different words: sad, angry, scared, excited, numb, relieved, lonely, tender…
  2. Wait after each one. Does it land in your body, or bounce off?
  3. When something resonates — even slightly — notice where in your body you checked. That's one of the places feelings live in you.
  4. Look into that place. What else is there?

Numb, blank, and empty are all feelings. They often function as protectors — keeping you from what's underneath. Be patient with them.

The more precisely you can name what you feel, the more agency you have. "Bad" is a start. "Disappointed and a little resentful because I expected more from myself" gives you something to actually work with.

  1. Use a feelings/sensations list to expand your vocabulary. The Hoffman Institute list is excellent.
  2. Try the How We Feel app (iOS + Android) for daily practice.
  3. When you notice a feeling, try to get more specific: not just "anxious" but "chest-tight, buzzy, anticipatory dread with a hint of excitement underneath."

If you're new to this, work through it with someone — a friend, therapist, coach, or even a supportive AI companion.

Sometimes you regulate and the feeling passes — just a cloud in the sky. But sometimes emotional residue sticks. You're still believing the feeling: "I feel hopeless, so there must be no hope." That's identification.

Two options when this happens:

  1. Regulate harder. Use your regulation tools until you have enough resource to face what's underneath. There's no shame in this — it's strategic.
  2. Face into it. If you have the tools, support, and capacity — go to the root. What belief is the feeling sitting on? What does it mean about you? Resolve that, and the feeling loses its stickiness.
Common core patterns: Fear ("What's the bad thing you think would happen?") · Rejection/Inadequacy (not trying because you're afraid to fail) · Hopelessness (not trying because you believe it won't work) · Helplessness ("I don't know how, I can't")

Regulation

Activities that reliably turn down the volume on internal intensity. These are not resolving the cause — they're helping you get through it with enough resource to either keep going or face into it later.

Create two lists. Be honest with both.

  1. Healthy regulation: activities that reduce your activation without negative side effects. Walking, cold water, breathwork, cooking, dancing, calling a friend, journaling, cleaning, being in nature.
  2. Unhealthy regulation: activities that reduce activation but cost you something. Scrolling, substances, overworking, isolating, binge eating, numbing out. These aren't "bad" — they're information. Notice when you reach for them and what they're substituting for.
  3. Keep both lists visible. When you're activated, you rarely have the bandwidth to think of what to do — the list does the thinking for you.

Somatic Tools

Your body is not separate from this work — it's where the work lives. These are hands-on, body-level practices you can do anytime.

Physical touch that helps your nervous system settle. These are co-regulation moves you can do solo.

  1. Face: Cup your cheeks. Hand to forehead. Hand to lips. Hands on the sides of the neck.
  2. Hold: Self-hug — wrap your arms around yourself. Hold your own sides.
  3. Soothe: Gently brush your legs with your hands. Apply gentle pressure or tender touch wherever feels right.
  4. Contain: Place a hand on your chest and one on your belly. Feel the containment of your own hands.
  1. Imagine someone you love. Let the feeling of caring for them arise.
  2. Imagine someone who loves you. Let the feeling of being cared for arise.
  3. Feel the feeling of love itself — not directed at anyone, just the quality of it.
  4. Direct it toward yourself. Toward the parts of you that are afraid, stuck, or hurting.

This isn't about manufacturing emotion. It's about finding what's already there and letting it reach the places that need it.
